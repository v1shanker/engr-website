PRINCIPLED ENTREPRENEURIAL DECISIONS
— Winter 2020 — 

Mondays 4:30 pm - 7:20 pm

Location: THORNTON 110

3 units

 

This course will challenge the next generation of business leaders and entrepreneurs to think critically about how their personal values and principles will inform the difficult decisions they will have to make as they grow their organization. The course will first equip students with frameworks to crystalize their own values and principles, from both personal and organizational perspectives. Students will learn to apply their own core values through moral reasoning techniques presented in lecture. A selection of readings and case studies will provide students with tangible examples of the challenges other prominent entrepreneurs have faced. Each class will be highly immersive, featuring conversations with entrepreneurial guest speakers, role-play scenarios, and facilitated break-out sessions. Through conversations with each other and the real case protagonists, students will leave the class more prepared to navigate the ethical dilemmas that they may encounter during their professional lives.

DESIRED LEARNING OUTCOME

  • Provide an overview of certain frameworks for moral reasoning for individuals, organizations, and communities 
  • Develop actionable moral sensitivity to identify and handle ethical dilemmas early on, arming students with tools and pattern recognition
  • Enable students to develop their own customized framework, codifying their principles in order to provide guardrails in a broad range of difficult entrepreneurial dilemmas

 

TARGET STUDENTS

  • Graduate students and undergraduate students, specifically those who can relate to pursuing an entrepreneurial opportunity
  • Preferred: Students with prior exposure to challenging dilemmas in the workplace or those who have completed one or more project-based entrepreneurship courses (though not required)

 

CLASS FORMAT

Before each class, students will be assigned readings and a case study. The instructor will begin class by synthesizing the main takeaways from the last class and latest readings. The protagonist from each week’s case will join the class as a guest speaker. During class, the instructor will facilitate a conversation to unpack the case’s underlying ethical dilemma. Students will have the opportunity to role play and consider different ways in which they may have approached the situation, informed by their own set of principles. The case protagonist will listen to the student discussion and then provide commentary on his or her personal experience and decision-making process. Occasionally there will be break-out sessions, in which students will have the opportunity to expand upon the case discussion and apply their own principles to analogue situations.

 

ASSIGNMENT DESCRIPTIONS

  1. Weekly Case and Framework Analysis: Students will be evaluated on a “check” or “plus” basis across the following assignments:
    1. Review the weekly background portion of the case, answering questions
    2. Additional weekly assignments that will provide building blocks for individual and company principles frameworks
  2. Capstone assignment (4-parts)
    1. Part 1 will be a series of exercises and questionnaires to help every individual team member to analyze their personal values and principles at the beginning of the quarter.
    2. During parts 2-4 students will form groups that represent company leadership teams. Instructors will present each group with business dilemmas from the real world. Groups will be challenged to crystalize their processes for decision-making and conflict resolution, as well as reflect on how their individual perspectives can be reconciled to forge a set of shared team values and principles. Teams will also design a set of hands-on management protocols which reflects their set of principles.

 

EVALUATION & GRADING

  • 25% In-class participation
  • 25% Weekly, individual homework assignments assignments
  • 25% Pre-work and reflection on our class session with Ray Dalio
  • 25% Capstone assignment
